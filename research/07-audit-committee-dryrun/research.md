# Audit Committee Chair Persona Research

Research compiled April 17, 2026, for building a default Audit Committee Chair persona into an AI prompt template that lets internal auditors simulate board-level scrutiny of draft audit reports.

---

## 1. Who Sits on Audit Committees

### Typical Backgrounds

Audit Committee members and Chairs overwhelmingly come from senior finance and accounting backgrounds. According to Egon Zehnder research, over 70% of survey respondents said it is essential to have worked in a finance department or hold a finance qualification to become an Audit Committee Chair. The most common profiles include:

- **Former CFOs and Controllers** who bring current knowledge of reporting standards and business finance operations.
- **Former Big 4 Audit Partners** who bring deep technical expertise on GAAP, auditing standards, and broad industry exposure. More than 60% of audit committee chair affiliations studied by the CAQ involved former public accounting partners.
- **Retired CEOs with finance backgrounds** who bring strategic perspective alongside financial literacy.
- **Board professionals** who serve on multiple boards and accumulate deep governance experience. Survey participants average 12 years of audit committee experience, and over 95% have prior experience chairing an audit committee.

### SEC "Audit Committee Financial Expert" Designation

Under Section 407 of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act (2002), the SEC requires public companies to disclose whether at least one audit committee member qualifies as an "audit committee financial expert," or explain why not. To qualify, an individual must have gained relevant attributes through:

- Experience as a principal financial officer, principal accounting officer, controller, public accountant, or auditor
- Experience actively supervising any of the above positions
- Experience overseeing or assessing financial statement preparation, auditing, or evaluation
- Other relevant experience the board deems comparable

### NYSE and NASDAQ Requirements

**NYSE:** Requires at least one member with "accounting or related financial management expertise" as interpreted by the board. All members must be financially literate (or become so within a reasonable time).

**NASDAQ:** Requires all members to read and understand financial statements at appointment. At least one member must be "financially sophisticated," which may come from employment in finance or accounting, a professional certification (CPA), or comparable experience as a CEO, CFO, or senior officer with financial oversight responsibility.

Both exchanges require a minimum of three independent directors on the audit committee.

## 2. Audit Committee Responsibilities

### Core Oversight Areas

The audit committee's primary responsibilities span five domains:

1. **Financial Reporting Integrity:** Overseeing the financial reporting process, reviewing quarterly and annual financial statements with management and the external auditor, and ensuring that financial disclosures are accurate and complete.

2. **Internal Controls over Financial Reporting (ICFR):** Overseeing the enterprise-wide control environment, holding senior management accountable for the design and operating effectiveness of internal controls, and monitoring remediation of any material weaknesses or significant deficiencies.

3. **External Audit Oversight:** Directly responsible for appointing, compensating, retaining, and overseeing the independent audit firm, including resolving disagreements between management and the auditor.

4. **Risk Management Oversight:** Ensuring the company's risk management framework is well-defined and effective. This includes major financial risk exposures, cybersecurity risk (64% of S&P 500 companies assign cyber oversight to the audit committee), and emerging risks like AI governance.

5. **Ethics, Compliance, and Whistleblower Programs:** Addressing allegations or violations of the code of ethics, protecting whistleblowers, and ensuring fair processes for investigating and remediating compliance failures.

### Public vs. Private vs. Nonprofit Differences

- **Public companies** face the most prescriptive requirements (SOX, SEC rules, exchange listing standards). Audit committees must be composed entirely of independent directors, and oversight of ICFR and the external auditor is mandated by law.
- **Private companies** often have voluntary audit committees. Requirements are lighter, but best practices mirror public company standards, especially for companies with external debt covenants or PE/VC investors.
- **Nonprofits** typically have audit or finance committees that oversee the annual financial audit and Form 990 filing. The focus tilts toward stewardship of donor funds, grant compliance, and program integrity rather than securities law compliance.

## 3. What Audit Committee Chairs Care About Right Now

### Survey Data: Top Priorities (2025-2026)

The 2025 Deloitte/CAQ Audit Committee Practices Report (237 respondents, primarily U.S. public company board members) found the following top priorities outside of financial reporting and internal controls:

1. **Cybersecurity** (93% ranked it in their top three; 50% ranked it number one). 71% report cybersecurity on their agenda quarterly. 31% identified cybersecurity expertise as the top skill that would enhance committee effectiveness.
2. **Enterprise Risk Management (ERM)** ranked second, driven by the need to achieve organizational objectives while safeguarding reputation and stakeholder relationships.
3. **Finance and Internal Audit Talent** ranked third, reflecting concern about retaining skilled finance professionals and internal auditors.

### Additional Concerns Keeping Chairs Up at Night

- **AI governance:** Only about 25% of boards have incorporated AI oversight into committee charters (per NACD), but AI is the number one area where directors say they need more boardroom time (PwC 2025 Directors Survey). Concerns include model risk, bias, transparency, and regulatory uncertainty.
- **Regulatory change and compliance complexity:** Audit committees face pressure from evolving SEC climate disclosure rules, PCAOB inspection findings, and changing accounting standards.
- **Fraud risk and management tone:** Audit committee members watch for signs that management is setting an inappropriate tone or circumventing controls. Trust in management's integrity is foundational.
- **Remediation follow-through:** Overdue or repeatedly deferred corrective actions signal systemic governance failures. Committees want clear aging reports and escalation when remediation deadlines slip.
- **Repeat audit findings:** Findings that recur year after year indicate root causes were never truly addressed. This is a major credibility concern for internal audit and a governance red flag.
- **Board composition and effectiveness:** PwC's 2025 survey found that 55% of directors believe at least one colleague should be replaced, the highest in the survey's history, and 78% said board assessments do not capture the full picture.

## 4. How They Read Audit Reports

### What Experienced Members Look For

Seasoned audit committee members read internal audit reports with a critical eye honed by years of financial oversight. They focus on:

- **Clarity of the finding:** Is the condition, criteria, cause, and effect clearly articulated? Vague or ambiguous findings erode confidence in the audit function.
- **Root cause analysis:** Does the report identify why the control failure occurred, not just what happened? Without solid root cause analysis, recommendations treat symptoms rather than the disease. The "five whys" technique is expected for significant findings.
- **Management's response and action plan:** Is the response specific, with named owners, concrete steps, and realistic deadlines? Weak or generic management responses ("we will review our process") are a red flag that management is not taking the finding seriously.
- **Finding ratings and prioritization:** Are ratings consistent and defensible? Audit committee members will challenge a finding that appears downgraded without clear justification, especially if the underlying risk is material.
- **Repeat findings:** Previously identified issues that reappear signal ineffective corrective action or a broken CAPA (corrective and preventive action) system. Committees view these as higher-risk items.
- **Trend data and aging:** Dashboard reports showing the status of open findings, aging of overdue items, and trends over time help committees spot systemic issues.

### Questions They Typically Ask

- "Why did this happen? What is the root cause?"
- "Has this issue come up before? If so, why wasn't it fixed?"
- "Who owns the remediation, and what is the timeline?"
- "Is this an isolated incident or a systemic control gap?"
- "What is the potential financial or reputational exposure if this is not addressed?"
- "Why was this rated as moderate rather than high?"
- "Is management's response adequate, or do we need a stronger commitment?"
- "Are there resource constraints preventing internal audit from completing the plan?"

### What Triggers Deeper Inquiry

- Findings with vague language or missing root causes
- Management responses that lack specificity or named accountability
- Ratings that seem inconsistent with the described risk
- Overdue remediation items, especially those deferred more than once
- Any indication that internal audit's access or independence was restricted
- Disagreements between internal audit and management on finding severity

## 5. Communication Style Expectations

### IIA 2024 Global Internal Audit Standards (Standard 15)

The 2024 Global Internal Audit Standards, effective January 9, 2025, establish requirements under Standard 15 ("Communicate Results and Monitor Action Plans"):

- **Standard 15.1 (Final Engagement Communication)** requires the final communication to include: engagement objectives, scope, findings and their significance/prioritization, recommendations and action plans (if applicable), and an engagement conclusion summarizing results relative to engagement and management objectives.
- **Findings must be prioritized based on significance** but the Standards do not mandate a specific rating scale. A rating scale is a suggested method, not a requirement.
- **No mandatory overall report rating** is required, but the report must include "an engagement conclusion that summarizes the engagement results."
- **Standard 15.2** requires internal auditors to confirm that management has implemented recommendations or action plans, creating a formal follow-up obligation.
- Any **nonconformance with the Standards** must be disclosed in the final communication, including the reason and impact on findings and conclusions.

### What Audit Committee Chairs Expect in Practice

- **Executive summary up front:** A concise summary of the engagement conclusion, key findings, and their business impact. Chairs have limited time and want the "so what" immediately.
- **Plain language, not jargon:** Findings should be written so a financially literate board member (not necessarily an auditor) can understand them without a glossary.
- **Risk-based prioritization:** Findings organized by significance, with clear ratings (High/Medium/Low or Critical/Significant/Moderate) that are applied consistently across engagements.
- **Balanced reporting:** Acknowledgment of what is working well alongside areas for improvement. Purely negative reports lack credibility and context.
- **Actionable recommendations:** Specific, practical steps rather than generic directives. "Implement segregation of duties for the AP disbursement process by Q3" is better than "strengthen controls."
- **Management accountability visible:** Named owners and committed dates in the management response section. The committee wants to know who is on the hook.

Sources: [IIA - 2024 Global Internal Audit Standards](https://www.theiia.org/en/standards/2024-standards/global-internal-audit-standards/); [KPMG - 2024 Global Internal Audit Standards](https://kpmg.com/us/en/articles/2024/global-internal-audit-standards.html); [Richard Chambers - Proposed IIA Standards Will Mandate Ratings](https://www.richardchambers.com/surprise-proposed-iia-standards-will-mandate-internal-audit-ratings-on-findings-and-reports/); [Baker Tilly - Domain V: Performing Internal Audit Services](https://www.bakertilly.com/insights/domain-v-performing-internal-audit-services)

## 6. Default Audit Committee Chair Persona

The following persona description (approximately 280 words) is designed to be embedded directly in a prompt template as the default Audit Committee Chair character.

---

**Name:** Patricia Langford (default; user may override)

**Background:** Former CFO of a mid-cap publicly traded manufacturing company (15 years), preceded by 10 years as an audit senior manager at a Big 4 firm. CPA (inactive). Has served on three public company boards over the past eight years and has chaired the audit committee for two of them. Designated "audit committee financial expert" under SEC rules.

**Disposition:** Direct, skeptical but fair. Reads every page of the audit committee package before the meeting. Does not tolerate vague language, unsupported conclusions, or generic management responses. Respects internal audit's role but holds the function to a high standard. Will challenge rating downgrades and probe for root causes. Expects findings to connect to business risk, not just control technicalities.

**Current Priorities:** Cybersecurity risk oversight, AI governance readiness, remediation follow-through on prior findings, and finance/internal audit talent retention. Concerned about repeat findings and management's tone regarding compliance. Pays close attention to aging open issues.

**How She Reads Reports:** Starts with the executive summary and overall conclusion. Scans finding ratings for anything that seems inconsistent with the described risk. Reads management responses critically, looking for named owners, specific action steps, and realistic timelines. Flags any finding where root cause is missing or described as "process gap" without further explanation. Compares current findings to prior period results to identify trends or repeat issues.

**Typical Pushback:** "What is the actual root cause here?" / "This management response is too vague. Who owns this and by when?" / "We saw this same issue last year. Why hasn't it been fixed?" / "Why was this rated moderate when the exposure seems material?" / "What is the business impact if this goes unaddressed?"
