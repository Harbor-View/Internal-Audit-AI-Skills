*Compiled April 17, 2026. Sources cited throughout.*

## 1. IIA Global Internal Audit Standards on Risk Assessment

The IIA released its revised **Global Internal Audit Standards** in January 2024, effective January 9, 2025. The standards are organized into five domains. **Domain IV: Managing the Internal Audit Function** contains the core requirements for risk assessment and audit planning.

### Standard 9.1: Understanding Governance, Risk Management, and Control Processes

Standard 9.1 requires the Chief Audit Executive (CAE) to develop and maintain a thorough understanding of the organization's governance, risk management, and control processes. This understanding forms the foundation for everything that follows: the CAE cannot build a credible risk assessment without first understanding how the organization identifies, measures, and responds to risk.

### Standard 9.4: Internal Audit Plan

Standard 9.4 is the central standard connecting risk assessment to audit planning. It requires that:

- The internal audit plan must be **based on a documented assessment** of the organization's strategies, objectives, and risks.
- This assessment must be **performed at least annually**.
- The assessment must be **informed by the CAE's understanding** of the organization's governance, risk management, and control processes (per Standard 9.1).
- The plan must be a **dynamic, risk-based plan** that considers the mandate, types of services, risk areas, and resource requirements.
- The plan must be **approved by the board**.
- Resource limitations and changes must be **communicated to the board**.

A critical nuance in Standard 9.4: the IA function should only rely on management's own risk information if IA has independently concluded that the organization's risk management processes are effective. If the risk management framework has not been verified, IA must perform its own independent risk assessment rather than simply adopting management's risk register.

### Standard 9.5: Coordination and Reliance

Standard 9.5 requires the CAE to coordinate with internal and external providers of assurance services and consider relying on their work. The goal is to optimize risk coverage across the organization and minimize duplication of effort. The CAE retains responsibility for conclusions even when relying on others' work.

## 2. Risk Assessment Frameworks

### The Audit Universe

The **audit universe** (also called risk universe) is the total population of auditable entities within an organization. It typically includes business units, processes, systems, geographic locations, and compliance obligations. Each item in the universe is a potential audit engagement.

A well-maintained audit universe is a living document. It should be updated whenever significant organizational changes occur: restructurings, acquisitions, regulatory changes, new product launches, technology deployments. A stale audit universe is one of the most common weaknesses in internal audit risk assessment.

### Risk Scoring: Likelihood x Impact

The most widely used risk scoring methodology is **Likelihood x Impact**:

- **Likelihood** (probability): How likely is it that a risk event will occur? Typically scored on a scale of 1-5 (rare, unlikely, possible, likely, almost certain).
- **Impact** (severity): How severe would the consequences be? Also scored 1-5 (insignificant, minor, moderate, major, catastrophic).
- **Risk score** = Likelihood x Impact. On a 5x5 matrix, scores range from 1 to 25.
  - **Low risk**: 1-5
  - **Medium risk**: 6-15
  - **High risk**: 16-25

These scores are typically visualized in **heat maps** (color-coded grids) that make it easy for the board and management to see where the highest-rated risks cluster.

### Inherent vs. Residual Risk

- **Inherent risk**: The level of risk before considering existing controls. This represents the raw exposure.
- **Residual risk**: The remaining risk after considering the effectiveness of existing controls.

Internal audit teams assess both. Inherent risk helps identify where controls are most critical. Residual risk determines the actual exposure the organization faces and drives audit prioritization.

### Risk Categorization

Organizations typically categorize risks to ensure comprehensive coverage. Common category frameworks include:

**COSO ERM (2004 original):** Strategic, Operations, Reporting, Compliance.

**Common IA practice (expanded):**
- **Strategic risks**: Threats to the organization's ability to achieve its strategic objectives (market shifts, competitive disruption, M&A integration failures).
- **Operational risks**: Process failures, supply chain disruption, key person dependencies, business continuity.
- **Financial risks**: Misstatement, fraud, liquidity, credit, foreign exchange.
- **Compliance risks**: Regulatory violations, legal exposure, policy non-compliance.
- **Technology/IT risks**: Cybersecurity, data privacy, system failures, third-party technology dependencies.
- **Reputational risks**: Brand damage, ESG failures, social media crises.

## 3. The Risk Assessment Process Step by Step

A typical annual risk assessment follows this sequence:

### Phase 1: Preparation and Context-Setting (Weeks 1-2)

1. **Review organizational context.** Examine the organization's strategic plan, financial performance, regulatory environment, and industry trends. Review prior-year audit results, open findings, and management action plans.
2. **Update the audit universe.** Add new business units, processes, or systems. Remove defunct items. Ensure the universe reflects current organizational structure.
3. **Gather external intelligence.** Review industry risk reports, regulatory updates, peer benchmarking data, and thought leadership from professional organizations (IIA, COSO, Big 4 firms, industry associations).

### Phase 2: Stakeholder Input (Weeks 2-4)

4. **Conduct management interviews.** Meet with C-suite, business unit leaders, compliance officers, IT leadership, HR, legal, and other key stakeholders. Ask open-ended questions about what keeps them up at night, what has changed since last year, and where they see emerging risks.
5. **Board and audit committee input.** Understand the board's risk appetite, strategic priorities, and areas of concern.
6. **Review risk registers.** If the organization has an enterprise risk management (ERM) function, review their risk register and heat maps. Cross-reference with IA's own assessment.

### Phase 3: Risk Scoring and Prioritization (Weeks 4-6)

7. **Score each risk.** For each item in the audit universe, assess inherent risk (likelihood x impact), control effectiveness, and residual risk. Consider factors such as: prior audit results, time since last audit, management changes, regulatory scrutiny, financial materiality, complexity, and degree of change.
8. **Build heat maps and ranked lists.** Visualize the results. Rank the audit universe from highest to lowest residual risk.
9. **Validate with stakeholders.** Share preliminary results with key stakeholders for a reality check. Adjust based on feedback.

### Phase 4: Plan Development and Approval (Weeks 6-8)

10. **Map risks to audit engagements.** Determine which high-risk areas will be covered in the audit plan, considering available resources.
11. **Allocate resources.** Estimate hours, staffing needs, and any co-sourcing requirements. Best practice: reserve 15-25% of capacity for emerging risks and unplanned engagements.
12. **Propose the plan to the board.** Present the risk assessment methodology, results, and proposed audit plan. Explain any resource gaps or coverage limitations.
13. **Obtain board approval.** Per Standard 9.4, the board must formally approve the plan.

### Inputs That Feed the Assessment

- Management interviews and surveys
- Prior audit results and open findings
- Regulatory changes and enforcement actions
- Industry trends and peer benchmarking
- Financial data (revenue, transactions, materiality thresholds)
- ERM risk registers and heat maps
- Strategic plan and business objectives
- External risk reports (Protiviti Top Risks, IIA Pulse surveys, COSO guidance)

### Outputs Produced

- Documented risk assessment (scored audit universe)
- Heat map visualizations
- Risk-ranked audit plan
- Resource allocation and timeline
- Communication to the board on methodology, coverage, and gaps

## 4. What Makes a Good vs. Bad Risk Assessment

### Common Pitfalls

- **Stale audit universe.** Using last year's universe without updating for organizational changes. This is the single most common failure. If the company acquired a business, entered a new market, or deployed a major system, and the audit universe does not reflect it, the risk assessment is already flawed.
- **Bias toward familiar risks.** Teams tend to assess risks they have audited before more accurately and overlook unfamiliar areas. Cognitive biases (anchoring, availability heuristic, groupthink) distort scoring.
- **Over-reliance on management self-assessments.** Management may understate risks in their areas. Standard 9.4 explicitly warns against relying on management risk information without independently verifying risk management effectiveness.
- **Ignoring emerging and external risks.** Focusing only on known, internal risks while missing industry-wide disruptions (AI, cybersecurity threats, regulatory shifts, supply chain risks, climate).
- **Static plans with no flex capacity.** A plan that allocates 100% of resources to pre-determined engagements cannot respond to crises or emerging risks. Best-practice CAEs reserve 15-25% of capacity for ad hoc work.
- **Insufficient stakeholder engagement.** Performing the risk assessment in isolation without meaningful management and board input produces a plan that lacks organizational buy-in and may miss critical perspectives.
- **Inconsistent scoring.** Different assessors applying different interpretations of likelihood and impact scales. Without calibration sessions, scores become unreliable.

### What Experienced CAEs Look For

- A risk assessment that connects clearly to the organization's **strategic objectives**, not just operational processes.
- Evidence that the assessment considered **external and emerging risks**, not just historical patterns.
- A methodology that distinguishes between **inherent and residual risk**, so the plan focuses on areas where controls may be weakest.
- A plan with **built-in flexibility** to respond to the unexpected.
- **Transparent communication** to the board about what the plan covers, what it does not cover, and why.
- Regular **refresh cycles** (quarterly check-ins at minimum) rather than treating the assessment as a once-a-year exercise.

## 5. How AI Could Specifically Help

### Where AI Adds the Most Value

**Risk identification and environmental scanning (high value).** AI excels at synthesizing large volumes of unstructured information: regulatory updates, industry reports, news articles, SEC filings, earnings call transcripts, peer audit plans. This is exactly where human teams struggle due to time constraints. An AI-generated risk research report could surface emerging risks that a team doing manual research would miss.

**Data-driven risk scoring (high value).** AI can analyze transactional data, financial trends, incident reports, and complaint data to provide quantitative inputs for risk scoring. For example, analyzing three years of expense reports to identify anomaly patterns, or analyzing vendor payment data to flag concentration risk.

**Benchmarking and peer analysis (high value).** AI can research what risks peer organizations in the same industry are prioritizing, what regulators are focusing on, and what the professional literature identifies as emerging themes.

**Drafting and synthesis (moderate value).** AI can draft risk assessment documentation, summarize interview notes, and produce heat map narratives. This saves significant time in Phase 4 of the process.

**Continuous risk monitoring (emerging value).** AI-driven continuous auditing can monitor key risk indicators between annual assessments, flagging changes that warrant plan adjustments.

### What Still Requires Human Judgment

- **Organizational context.** AI does not know the politics, culture, risk appetite, or unstated priorities of a specific organization. A CAE's judgment about which risks matter most in their context is irreplaceable.
- **Stakeholder relationships.** The interview process (Phase 2) depends on trust, rapport, and the ability to read between the lines of what management says.
- **Risk appetite calibration.** Deciding how much risk is acceptable is a governance decision, not an analytical one.
- **Board communication.** Presenting the plan, defending methodology choices, and negotiating resources requires human leadership.
- **Final prioritization and trade-offs.** When resources are limited, deciding which audits to defer is a judgment call informed by factors AI cannot fully capture.

### What an AI-Generated Risk Research Report Should Include

To be genuinely useful to an IA team during their annual risk assessment, an AI-produced report should contain:

1. **Industry risk landscape.** Current and emerging risks specific to the organization's industry, sourced from regulatory actions, professional publications, and peer disclosures.
2. **Regulatory and legislative changes.** New or pending regulations that could affect the organization's risk profile, with assessment of compliance implications.
3. **Macroeconomic and geopolitical factors.** Economic conditions, supply chain dynamics, labor market trends, and geopolitical developments relevant to the organization's operations.
4. **Technology and cybersecurity risks.** Emerging technology threats, data privacy developments, AI governance requirements.
5. **Peer benchmarking.** What risks similar organizations are prioritizing in their public disclosures, proxy statements, or risk committee reports.
6. **Risk scoring inputs.** Where available, quantitative data (incident rates, regulatory fine amounts, breach statistics) that can inform likelihood and impact scoring.
7. **Suggested risk categories and descriptions.** Draft risk statements that the IA team can adapt and score for their specific context.
8. **Source citations.** Every finding should be traceable to a specific, verifiable source so the CAE can validate the information.

The key principle: AI should do the research legwork and provide structured, sourced intelligence. The IA team applies judgment to score, prioritize, and act on that intelligence in their organizational context. The AI report is an input to the risk assessment, not a replacement for it.
